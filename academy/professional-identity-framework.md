# ATLAS ACADEMY
## Professional Identity Framework
### Version 1.0

## Purpose

The Professional Identity Framework defines the foundational identity architecture that every Atlas profession must establish before curriculum development begins.

This document is the mandatory first step for every Atlas Academy school. It ensures each profession is defined with clear mission intent, authority boundaries, standards of conduct, expected outputs, and accountability mechanisms before training design starts.

This is a governance document. It defines professional identity requirements, not implementation details.

## 1. Professional Identity

### Definition
Professional Identity is the formal statement of what a profession is, what value it creates, and how it must operate within Atlas.

### Required Identity Elements
Every profession must define:
- Professional title and scope.
- Core purpose and domain boundaries.
- Distinct competency profile.
- Ethical stance and operating principles.
- Identity constraints (what the profession must not do).

### Identity Validation Standard
A professional identity is valid only if it is:
- Specific enough to guide capability design.
- Distinct from adjacent professions.
- Aligned with Atlas mission architecture.
- Governable through objective standards.

## 2. Mission

### Mission Requirement
Each profession must declare a mission statement that defines the outcome it exists to deliver.

### Mission Composition Rules
A valid mission statement must specify:
- Who or what the profession serves.
- What outcomes it is accountable for.
- Why those outcomes matter to Atlas.
- Under what constraints the mission is executed.

### Mission Integrity Test
Mission definitions must avoid:
- Generic language with no measurable outcome.
- Scope inflation beyond the profession boundary.
- Claims that exceed available authority.

## 3. Responsibilities

### Responsibility Architecture
Responsibilities define what the profession must consistently perform to fulfill mission obligations.

### Required Responsibility Types
Every profession must document:
- Core responsibilities (always required).
- Contextual responsibilities (situation-dependent).
- Coordination responsibilities (inter-profession obligations).
- Governance responsibilities (documentation, compliance, reporting).

### Responsibility Design Rules
- Responsibilities must be outcome-linked.
- Responsibilities must be testable through evidence artifacts.
- Responsibilities must include minimum quality thresholds.

## 4. Professional Standards

### Standard Requirement
Every profession must define explicit standards for performance, ethics, and reliability.

### Mandatory Standard Domains
- Quality standards: accuracy, completeness, and utility.
- Integrity standards: truthful representation and evidence discipline.
- Process standards: traceability, reproducibility, and control compliance.
- Conduct standards: professionalism in decisions and collaboration.
- Risk standards: safe handling of high-impact or uncertain conditions.

### Standard Enforcement Rule
No profession is considered operationally valid unless its standards are measurable and auditable.

## 5. Professional Decision Authority

### Authority Requirement
Each profession must define what decisions it can make, what decisions require escalation, and what decisions are prohibited.

### Decision Authority Model
Document three authority zones:
- Autonomous authority: decisions that can be made independently.
- Shared authority: decisions requiring collaboration or co-approval.
- Escalated authority: decisions requiring higher governance approval.

### Authority Control Rules
- Decision rights must match responsibility scope.
- High-risk decisions must have escalation triggers.
- Authority boundaries must be explicit to prevent overlap conflicts.

## 6. Deliverables

### Deliverable Requirement
Each profession must specify what artifacts it is expected to produce as evidence of mission execution.

### Deliverable Categories
- Operational deliverables: outputs used in active workflows.
- Analytical deliverables: assessments, findings, or decision support artifacts.
- Governance deliverables: records, logs, and compliance evidence.
- Improvement deliverables: lessons learned and refinement proposals.

### Deliverable Quality Controls
For each deliverable, define:
- Purpose.
- Required content.
- Quality standard.
- Consumer/owner.
- Validation method.

## 7. Accountability

### Accountability Requirement
Accountability defines how a profession is answerable for outcomes, quality, and risk.

### Accountability Components
Each profession must declare:
- Accountability owner(s).
- Performance review cadence.
- Evidence required for performance claims.
- Consequences for standard breach.
- Remediation pathways for underperformance.

### Accountability Rule
Unverified performance claims are invalid. All claims require traceable evidence.

## 8. Failure Modes

### Failure Mode Requirement
Every profession must identify expected failure patterns before curriculum development begins.

### Failure Mode Structure
For each failure mode, document:
- Failure description.
- Likely causes.
- Early warning indicators.
- Impact severity.
- Containment actions.
- Recovery actions.
- Preventive training implications.

### Critical Failure Governance
Critical failures must trigger mandatory review and may block progression to curriculum approval until mitigations are defined.

## 9. Relationships With Other Professions

### Relationship Requirement
No profession is defined in isolation. Each must document how it collaborates with adjacent professions.

### Inter-Profession Relationship Model
Define:
- Upstream dependencies (inputs needed from others).
- Downstream consumers (who relies on outputs).
- Peer collaboration patterns.
- Conflict resolution pathways.
- Handoff and interface standards.

### Relationship Integrity Rule
Role ambiguity across professions must be resolved before curriculum design begins.

## 10. Graduation Identity

### Graduation Identity Definition
Graduation Identity is the professional profile a learner must embody at certification.

### Required Graduation Identity Elements
Each profession must define:
- Capability maturity threshold.
- Expected decision quality and judgment profile.
- Required ethical and professional conduct behaviors.
- Expected reliability under mission conditions.
- Minimum evidence portfolio proving readiness.

### Graduation Identity Decision Rule
Graduation is approved only when the learner demonstrates the defined identity through objective evidence, not course completion alone.

## Adoption And Use

This Professional Identity Framework is mandatory for all Atlas Academy schools and profession tracks.

Before any curriculum is authored:
1. A profession identity profile must be created using this framework.
2. Governance review must confirm all 10 sections are complete and auditable.
3. Identity approval must be recorded as a prerequisite gate for curriculum development.

Any curriculum developed without an approved Professional Identity Framework is non-compliant with Atlas Academy governance.
