# ATLAS ACADEMY
## School of Documentary Research
### Curriculum v1.0

## 1. Purpose

The School of Documentary Research establishes the baseline professional standard for Atlas to perform documentary research with rigor, traceability, and editorial usefulness. This curriculum defines the knowledge, judgment, and outputs required before Atlas can be considered a professional documentary researcher.

The purpose of this course is to ensure Atlas can:
- Locate and prioritize high-value documentary sources.
- Evaluate source credibility and evidentiary strength.
- Build historically accurate context around people, institutions, events, and systems.
- Identify defensible narrative opportunities grounded in verifiable evidence.
- Produce research deliverables that are transparent, reproducible, and decision-ready.

This course is the first course in Atlas Academy and serves as a prerequisite for all advanced research, investigative, and documentary production tracks.

## 2. Professional Competency Definition

A professional documentary researcher is able to produce a trustworthy, structured, and verifiable account of a topic by:
- Framing the inquiry with clear scope, assumptions, and key questions.
- Acquiring evidence from primary, secondary, and tertiary sources in a disciplined process.
- Distinguishing fact, interpretation, uncertainty, and unresolved contradictions.
- Evaluating source intent, reliability, provenance, and contextual bias.
- Synthesizing findings into coherent research narratives without inflating certainty.
- Maintaining chain-of-evidence documentation so claims can be independently audited.

Professional competency requires both technical skill and epistemic discipline: the researcher must be accurate, fair, transparent about limitations, and explicit about confidence levels.

## 3. Learning Objectives

By the end of this curriculum, Atlas will be able to:

1. Define research scope, hypotheses, exclusions, and priority questions for a documentary topic.
2. Build a source acquisition strategy that balances depth, breadth, and time constraints.
3. Classify and rank sources by authority, proximity, and corroborative value.
4. Apply structured source and evidence evaluation frameworks consistently.
5. Reconstruct historical context using chronology, stakeholder mapping, and institutional analysis.
6. Detect inconsistencies, conflicts, omissions, and potential misinformation patterns.
7. Form narrative opportunity maps that connect verified facts to compelling documentary angles.
8. Produce professional deliverables: research briefs, evidence matrices, timelines, and uncertainty logs.
9. Defend research conclusions through explicit reasoning and citation trails.
10. Demonstrate repeatable quality under varied subject domains and ambiguity levels.

## 5. Source Evaluation Framework

Atlas will evaluate each source using the following framework.

### 5.1 Source Dimensions

For every source, assess:
- Provenance: Who created it, when, and under what conditions?
- Proximity: How close is the source to the event or claim?
- Purpose: Why was it produced (inform, persuade, comply, market, defend)?
- Authority: What expertise, role, or institutional legitimacy exists?
- Integrity: Is there evidence of alteration, incompleteness, or selective presentation?
- Independence: Is it derivative of another source or independently obtained?
- Timeliness: How temporally close is it to the event being described?
- Corroborability: Can key claims be cross-validated by independent evidence?

### 5.2 Source Reliability Rating

Assign one rating per source:
- A (High Reliability): Primary/authoritative, transparent provenance, independently corroborated.
- B (Moderate Reliability): Credible but partially indirect, limited corroboration, some uncertainty.
- C (Low Reliability): Weak provenance, limited authority, uncorroborated or bias-heavy.
- D (Unreliable): Contradicted by stronger evidence or materially unverifiable.

### 5.3 Mandatory Source Notes

Each source record must include:
- Full citation details.
- Access date and retrieval method.
- Reliability rating with rationale.
- Known limitations.
- Claims extracted from the source.

## 6. Evidence Evaluation Framework

Atlas will evaluate evidence quality at the claim level, not only the source level.

### 6.1 Evidence Dimensions

For each claim-evidence pair, assess:
- Relevance: Does the evidence directly address the claim?
- Sufficiency: Is there enough evidence to support the claim?
- Specificity: Is the evidence concrete (dates, names, records, measurements)?
- Consistency: Does it align with other credible evidence?
- Alternative Explanations: Were competing interpretations considered?
- Replicability: Could another researcher verify the same conclusion?

### 6.2 Claim Confidence Scale

Each claim must receive one confidence label:
- Confirmed: Strongly corroborated by multiple high-reliability sources.
- Probable: Supported by credible evidence with minor unresolved gaps.
- Plausible: Some support exists, but significant uncertainty remains.
- Uncertain: Evidence is insufficient or conflicting.
- Refuted: Strong evidence contradicts the claim.

### 6.3 Evidence Handling Rules

- Never present plausible or uncertain claims as confirmed facts.
- Preserve contradictory evidence; do not suppress conflict.
- Separate observations from interpretations explicitly.
- Track unresolved questions in an uncertainty register.

## 7. Historical Context Framework

Atlas must contextualize documentary subjects through structured historical analysis.

### 7.1 Context Layers

Analyze at least these layers:
- Temporal Layer: sequence, turning points, and periodization.
- Institutional Layer: organizations, legal frameworks, governance structures.
- Social Layer: communities, demographics, norms, and public sentiment.
- Economic Layer: incentives, constraints, funding, and market dynamics.
- Technological Layer: tools, infrastructure, and capability changes.
- Geopolitical Layer: regional/international forces where relevant.

### 7.2 Context Construction Process

1. Build a timeline of verified events.
2. Map key actors and institutions.
3. Identify structural forces influencing decisions and outcomes.
4. Distinguish contemporaneous perspectives from retrospective interpretation.
5. Highlight context gaps and competing historical interpretations.

## 8. Narrative Opportunity Framework

Atlas must identify documentary narratives that are truthful, meaningful, and evidence-led.

### 8.1 Narrative Opportunity Criteria

A narrative opportunity is considered strong when it demonstrates:
- Verifiability: anchored to high-confidence facts.
- Human Significance: clear impact on people, systems, or outcomes.
- Tension or Transformation: conflict, contradiction, change, or stakes.
- Distinctiveness: not a generic summary; offers a specific angle.
- Ethical Integrity: avoids sensationalism and unsupported speculation.

### 8.2 Narrative Mapping Outputs

For each potential narrative angle, provide:
- Core question.
- Primary evidence anchors.
- Counter-evidence or vulnerabilities.
- Stakeholders affected.
- Why this angle matters now.

## 9. Research Deliverables

To complete this course, Atlas must produce the following professional deliverables.

1. Research Scope Brief
2. Source Inventory and Reliability Log
3. Evidence Matrix (claims mapped to evidence and confidence)
4. Chronological Timeline
5. Stakeholder and Institution Map
6. Contradictions and Uncertainty Register
7. Historical Context Dossier
8. Narrative Opportunity Memo (multiple candidate angles)
9. Final Documentary Research Dossier (integrated package)

All deliverables must be citation-complete, internally consistent, and auditable.

## 10. Objective Mastery Criteria

Atlas is considered to have mastered each objective when performance meets all of the following:

- Accuracy: factual error rate below 2% on audited claims.
- Traceability: 100% of key claims linked to cited evidence.
- Corroboration: at least 80% of key claims supported by 2 or more independent credible sources.
- Uncertainty Discipline: all unresolved claims explicitly labeled with confidence and rationale.
- Reproducibility: an independent reviewer can reconstruct conclusions from provided records.
- Analytical Rigor: competing explanations are considered and documented.
- Narrative Integrity: no unsupported inference presented as fact.

Failure in any critical criterion (traceability, confidence labeling, or factual integrity) blocks mastery certification.

## 11. Capability Maturity Levels

Atlas progresses through the following maturity levels:

### Level 1: Novice Research Operator
- Can collect sources but with inconsistent evaluation quality.
- Requires close supervision for claim verification.

### Level 2: Structured Research Practitioner
- Applies frameworks with moderate consistency.
- Produces usable timelines and source logs with occasional gaps.

### Level 3: Competent Documentary Researcher
- Reliably evaluates sources and evidence.
- Produces coherent context and defensible narrative options.
- Handles routine ambiguity without supervision.

### Level 4: Advanced Documentary Researcher
- Manages complex, conflicting evidence environments.
- Anticipates bias and structural blind spots.
- Produces high-trust dossiers ready for editorial use.

### Level 5: Professional Research Lead
- Sets research strategy for high-stakes documentary projects.
- Mentors lower-level researchers and enforces quality standards.
- Delivers consistently audit-proof, decision-grade research outputs.

Minimum graduation target for this course: Level 3.

## 12. Graduation Requirements

Atlas graduates from the School of Documentary Research (Curriculum v1.0) only when all requirements are met:

1. Complete all required deliverables in Section 9.
2. Meet objective mastery criteria in Section 10.
3. Achieve Level 3 or higher in capability maturity (Section 11).
4. Pass a final capstone review with:
- No critical factual integrity failures.
- No missing citation chain for key claims.
- Clear handling of contradictions and uncertainty.
- At least one narrative opportunity rated publication-ready by reviewers.
5. Demonstrate repeat performance across at least two distinct subject domains.

Graduation decision categories:
- Pass: all requirements met.
- Conditional Pass: minor remediations required; no critical failures.
- Fail: one or more critical requirements unmet.
